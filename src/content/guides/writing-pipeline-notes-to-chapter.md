This guide presents a practical pipeline for doctoral writing: collect notes, assemble argument blocks, draft sections, and integrate them into chapter logic. The goal is to reduce restart friction and keep output consistent.

## Start with atomic notes

Each note should contain one claim, one source, one limitation and one possible reuse point.

## Build argument blocks

Group notes into small blocks answering one local question. A block usually needs a thesis line, two to three supporting references and one transition sentence.

## Draft sections from blocks

Turn each block into 300 to 600 words. Keep section scope narrow enough to finish in one or two sessions.

## Weekly control loop

- two writing sessions,
- one bibliography cleanup,
- one section-level review.

## Quality checks

- definitions are stable,
- claims are backed by references,
- transitions are explicit,
- chapter question is visible.

## FAQ

### Is word count the best metric?

No. A short coherent section with valid references is more valuable than a long unstable draft.

### When should editing happen?

Run a logic pass first, then a language pass. Mixing both usually slows progress.

### How to recover after a missed week?

Restart with one micro-output and rebuild cadence over two weeks.
