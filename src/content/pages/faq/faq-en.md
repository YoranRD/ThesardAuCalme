## Administration

### 1) What should be handled first after arrival?
Secure administrative identity first: enrollment pathway, residence-status tracking and a deadline log.
Without this base, later steps become slower and harder to verify.
Use one tracker only, not five scattered notes.
Review it weekly.

**Example:** one spreadsheet with columns "step", "deadline", "proof", "status".

### 2) Should admin tasks be completed before research starts?
No. Run both in parallel with separate blocks.
Admin-only weeks reduce scientific momentum.
Research-only weeks can create legal/process risk.
A balanced structure is more robust.

**Example:** two admin blocks and three research blocks per week.

### 3) How can document delays be reduced?
Prepare files early and keep timestamped proof of submission.
Ask for confirmation in writing.
Track pending items with one owner and one date.
Follow up with short, factual messages.

**Example:** "Submitted on March 4, could missing items be confirmed?"

### 4) What if two official sources conflict?
Do not guess.
Capture both sources and ask the relevant office directly.
Provide personal context to avoid generic answers.
Archive the final written clarification.

**Example:** send both links to international office with a targeted question.

### 5) How can deadlines be tracked without constant stress?
Centralize dates in one calendar.
Set one main reminder and one backup reminder.
Avoid multiplying tracking apps.
Keep a fixed weekly review.

**Example:** one calendar plus one tasks app is usually enough.

## Writing

### 6) How can writing continue during fragmented weeks?
Use short deep-work blocks with visible output goals.
Pre-load sessions the day before.
End each session with a next-action line.
Track output, not only effort.

**Example:** "Write one argument paragraph with two references in 45 minutes."

### 7) How many writing hours are needed weekly?
There is no universal number.
What matters is reliable output and decision quality.
Three strong sessions can outperform ten scattered hours.
Consistency beats intensity spikes.

**Example:** three 90-minute writing blocks every week.

### 8) How to convert notes into chapter text?
Use a stable pipeline: note -> paragraph -> section -> chapter.
Do not jump directly to full chapter drafting.
Ensure each note supports one precise claim.
Then group notes by argument logic.

**Example:** five notes become two paragraphs, then one subsection.

### 9) When is a section “good enough” to send?
When it answers a clear question.
When references are traceable and transitions are coherent.
When limitations are explicit.
When no major contradiction appears in a focused review.

**Example:** run a five-point checklist before sending to supervisor.

### 10) Is daily writing mandatory?
Not necessarily.
Predictable cadence is more important than daily volume.
Stable weekly rhythm usually sustains progress better.
Protect continuity of decisions.

**Example:** Monday, Wednesday, Friday writing schedule for 8 weeks.

## Tools

### 11) How should a reference manager be chosen?
Choose for reliability, traceability and export quality.
Test with a small real dataset first.
Check duplicates, metadata and citation style output.
Avoid major tool switching during critical phases.

**Example:** import 30 references and test citation output in a draft.

### 12) Is a complex note-taking system necessary?
No.
The main criterion is fast retrieval of useful notes.
Keep folder depth low and naming consistent.
Prefer boring, durable structure over novelty.

**Example:** folders for reading, chapters and admin plus status tags.

### 13) How to decide between free and paid tools?
Match decision to measurable need.
Compare privacy, export, collaboration and total cost.
Use free options when requirements are covered.
Move to paid only for proven gains.

**Example:** paid upgrade only after repeated collaboration bottlenecks.

### 14) Why is 3-2-1 backup critical for a thesis?
Because thesis files are high-value and long-horizon.
Single-point failure is unacceptable.
3-2-1 reduces systemic risk.
Restoration testing is part of the protocol.

**Example:** laptop + external disk + remote copy with monthly restore test.

### 15) How can AI tools be used responsibly?
Use AI for structure and language support, not evidence generation.
Verify every sensitive claim and reference.
Follow institutional disclosure policies.
Keep scientific judgment human-led.

**Example:** AI proposes outline; researcher validates all arguments and citations.

## Supervision

### 16) What if supervisor feedback is delayed?
Ask for targeted feedback instead of full-document review.
State one decision expected from the reading.
Propose a clear timeline.
Use polite, trackable follow-ups.

**Example:** "Please review intro + Figure 2 decision by March 20."

### 17) How should meetings be prepared?
Send a short agenda in advance.
Define two to three decisions expected.
Bring a one-page status note.
End with dated action items.

**Example:** 30-minute meeting with three explicit arbitration points.

### 18) How to handle conflicting supervisor advice?
Document differences neutrally.
Ask for decision criteria.
Present option A/B with consequences.
Archive final decision and rationale.

**Example:** one comparison table shared with both supervisors.

### 19) How often should progress updates happen?
Fixed cadence is better than random bursts.
Monthly strategic meeting plus short weekly written updates often works.
Keep updates decision-oriented.
Avoid status-only communication.

**Example:** weekly 8-line update and monthly structured meeting.

### 20) How to communicate delays professionally?
Communicate early.
Provide diagnosis and recovery plan.
Avoid long justification without action.
Propose revised milestones.

**Example:** "Deliverable delayed by 10 days, three-step catch-up plan attached."

## Mental load

### 21) How to stop feeling “always behind”?
Track concrete outputs weekly.
Break goals into observable deliverables.
Run a short Friday review.
Keep a progress log.

**Example:** three weekly outputs listed for six consecutive weeks.

### 22) What to do after a lost week?
Use a reset week protocol.
Set one primary output only.
Rebuild cadence before scope expansion.
Defer non-essential tasks.

**Example:** complete one short section before adding new obligations.

### 23) How to handle high-stress periods?
Temporarily reduce scope.
Protect sleep and baseline routines.
Seek support if warning signs persist.
Maintain at least minimal scientific output.

**Example:** reduce from five weekly goals to two during admin-heavy weeks.

### 24) How to sustain a routine over multiple years?
Build simple systems with periodic adjustment.
Avoid extreme routines that collapse quickly.
Accept variable intensity cycles.
Re-evaluate monthly.

**Example:** same weekly framework, adjusted load by period.

### 25) When should the doctoral plan be revised?
When bottlenecks repeat for more than two months.
When original assumptions no longer match constraints.
When delays accumulate without strategic change.
When supervision confirms need for redirection.

**Example:** quarterly strategic review with updated priorities and timeline.
