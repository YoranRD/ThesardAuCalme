## 1) École doctorale (doctoral school)
Institutional structure supervising doctoral progression, training obligations and part of administrative milestones.
It complements laboratory-level supervision.
**Example:** the doctoral school validates annual registration and training records.

## 2) Laboratoire / labo
Research unit where daily scientific work is anchored.
It provides environment, collaborators and technical support.
**Example:** data collection protocols are coordinated within the lab team.

## 3) HDR
Qualification enabling formal doctoral supervision in the French system context.
It reflects advanced supervisory responsibility.
**Example:** primary supervision is handled by a researcher with HDR.

## 4) Contrat doctoral
Funded doctoral employment contract over a defined period.
It sets rights, obligations and salary conditions.
**Example:** the doctoral contract defines workload and funding horizon.

## 5) Inscription administrative
Formal enrollment process for student/doctoral status.
It unlocks institutional access and legal continuity.
**Example:** some university services remain inactive before enrollment confirmation.

## 6) Soutenance (defense)
Final oral defense before a jury.
It evaluates contribution, methods and critical reasoning.
**Example:** defense rehearsal is timed and peer-reviewed before the final day.

## 7) Manuscript
Main thesis document organized into chapters and appendices.
It is the core written output of the doctorate.
**Example:** manuscript version is frozen before official submission.

## 8) Publication
Scientific article submitted to journal or conference.
Publications disseminate results and invite peer scrutiny.
**Example:** one chapter is converted into a publication manuscript.

## 9) Peer review
Evaluation by domain experts before publication.
It tests rigor, clarity and methodological soundness.
**Example:** reviewers request an additional comparative experiment.

## 10) Preprint
Public manuscript version before final peer-reviewed publication.
It accelerates dissemination with provisional status.
**Example:** preprint is uploaded after internal quality check.

## 11) Postprint
Accepted manuscript version after peer review, usually before publisher layout.
Sharing rules depend on policy conditions.
**Example:** postprint is deposited in institutional repository after embargo.

## 12) Impact factor
Journal-level bibliometric indicator.
It should not replace article-level quality assessment.
**Example:** a lower-impact venue may still be better aligned with target audience.

## 13) ORCID
Persistent researcher identifier linking works and profile.
It reduces ambiguity in author identity.
**Example:** ORCID is attached during submission metadata.

## 14) DOI
Permanent digital identifier for scholarly objects.
It supports stable citation and retrieval.
**Example:** missing DOI entries are corrected before bibliography freeze.

## 15) Bibliography
Structured list of cited and used sources.
It underpins traceability of claims.
**Example:** weekly bibliography cleanup prevents end-stage chaos.

## 16) Citation
Explicit reference to a source in text.
Correct citation prevents unattributed borrowing.
**Example:** each key methodological claim has at least one primary citation.

## 17) Appendix
Supplementary section containing detailed material.
It preserves evidence without overloading main text.
**Example:** full protocol details are moved to Appendix B.

## 18) Methodology
Framework of methods, assumptions and validation design.
It explains how results were generated.
**Example:** methodology section justifies metric choice and thresholds.

## 19) Hypothesis
Testable statement guiding analysis.
A useful hypothesis is falsifiable and measurable.
**Example:** H1 is tested across three independent datasets.

## 20) State of the art
Synthesis of current leading work in a field.
It positions contribution and identifies gaps.
**Example:** state-of-the-art review shows limited evaluation in real-world settings.

## 21) Literature review
Process of collecting, filtering and synthesizing relevant sources.
It supports theory and method choices.
**Example:** monthly review cycle focuses on top-priority papers.

## 22) CSI (individual monitoring committee)
Formal doctoral monitoring mechanism in many French contexts.
It reviews progress and research conditions.
**Example:** progress brief is submitted before CSI meeting.

## 23) Funding
Financial resources supporting doctoral activity.
Funding affects timeline and execution stability.
**Example:** travel budget is aligned with conference submission schedule.

## 24) Scholarship
Financial support granted under specific criteria.
It may complement or replace other funding streams.
**Example:** mobility scholarship funds short research stay abroad.

## 25) Postdoc
Post-doctoral research contract, often fixed-term.
It extends or redirects scientific specialization.
**Example:** postdoc applications start six months before defense.

## 26) Conference
Scientific event for presenting and discussing research.
In many fields it is also a primary publication channel.
**Example:** full paper submitted to annual flagship conference.

## 27) Workshop
Focused format around a specific topic, often exploratory.
It enables fast feedback and targeted networking.
**Example:** preliminary method is presented in thematic workshop.

## 28) Dataset
Data collection used for model training, testing or analysis.
Dataset quality strongly affects validity.
**Example:** inclusion criteria are documented in dataset note.

## 29) Reproducibility
Ability to reproduce results with same data and protocol.
It is a core scientific reliability criterion.
**Example:** analysis script is rerun in clean environment before submission.

## 30) Versioning
Structured management of document/code/figure versions.
Versioning prevents loss and inconsistency.
**Example:** Git tags mark each major submission state.

## 31) Abstract
Short summary of problem, method, results and scope.
Often the most-read section of a paper.
**Example:** abstract is rewritten after final results to avoid overclaiming.

## 32) Keywords
Search-oriented terms describing topic and method.
They improve discoverability and indexing.
**Example:** keywords are aligned with field-standard terminology.

## 33) Affiliation
Institutional identity of an author.
It contextualizes the research environment.
**Example:** affiliation line is standardized across all submissions.

## 34) Corresponding author
Author responsible for communication with editors and reviewers.
This role manages deadlines and revision logistics.
**Example:** corresponding author compiles point-by-point reviewer responses.

## 35) Plagiarism
Unattributed reuse of ideas, text or results.
It is a major ethical violation.
**Example:** paraphrased material includes explicit source citation.

## 36) Open access
Publication model allowing free reader access.
Implementation varies by publisher and policy.
**Example:** accepted version is shared under institutional open-access policy.

## 37) Embargo
Delay period before public sharing is allowed.
Common in publisher deposit rules.
**Example:** repository release scheduled after 12-month embargo.

## 38) Ethics committee
Body reviewing ethical compliance of research protocols.
Approval may be required before data collection.
**Example:** protocol submitted for ethics review before participant recruitment.

## 39) Informed consent
Documented participant agreement after clear information.
It is central in human-subject research integrity.
**Example:** signed consent is stored before interview begins.

## 40) Project viability
Practical ability of a research plan to remain executable.
It depends on scope, resources and timeline realism.
**Example:** scope is narrowed to keep 18-month plan feasible.
